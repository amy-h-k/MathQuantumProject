title: Superposition: The idea that something can exist in two states at once. Where did superposition come from? A look into both the mathematical buildup and the cultural precedent.

My motivation for this project was to display how nothing is truly isolated. Superposition does not only exist as a mathematical concept, but as a cultural notion. Math is a result of human thought, and too often we separate it from the humanities which define our societies, the context in which superposition is discovered and studied. 

### Mathematical Precedent!

Superpostion is a mathematical ordeal today, including: linear algebra, trigonometry, probability, circuitry, vectors, visualization, and experimentation.

The first person to propose superposition mathematically was Bernouli, who said that "The general motion of a vibrating system is given by a superposition of its proper vibrations." [here](https://ccrma.stanford.edu/~jos/sasp/Daniel_Bernoulli_s_Modal_Decomposition.html)


Bernouli's conjecture was disagreed with at the time, by the likes of Euler and LaGrange, but was later proven by Fournier. Fournier's analyisis reverse engineered overlapping waves, using superposition to explain that they were simply combinations of sine and cosine waves, remarkably simplifying wave functions, and putting Superposition as a mathematical concept on the map with real and practical usage. We later saw superposition advanced by physicists such as Schrodinger and Dirac. Dirac, often considered one of the founders of quantum mecahnics, formalized the superposition principle, and came up with bra-ket notation, which we still use today.

Erwin Schrodinger gave us both an equation and a cat. His equation, Schrodinger's Equation, (unexpected name, huh?), demonstrated how quantum states change over time, and its derivations led to some of the largest breakthroughs in wave mechanics and quantum physics as a whole science. Now, the cat.

One of the most well-known, practical explanations of superposition is Schrodinger's cat. This was crucial, because, even though Schrodinger considered it to be an example of the lunacy of quantum superposition, it proved one of the key principles of superposition, which would later set the stage for all of quantum computing: A state can exist in many states at once until measurement. The box became a metaphor for the unknown and unmeasured, and the cat demonstrated the practical implications of what quantum superposition could mean. ![cat_image](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Schrodingers_cat.svg)

### Cultural Precedent! 

But superposition as a concept, un-mathematically, existed long before any quantum notion. Let's turn the clock back from Bernouli. A couple thousand years or so, to Ancient Egypt. Where the underworld is presided over by the first man to have a (literal) Green thumb, Osiris. ![osiris](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Standing_Osiris.svg/500px-Standing_Osiris.svg.png)
Osiris is the Egyptian god of the dead, lord of the underworld. Osiris was killed by his brother, Set, and put back together by his wife Isis, though he was missing one piece. Osiris is simultaneously both dead and alive. He is dead in that he cannot leave the underworld, and will never return to the land of the living, yet he is alive in that he is still ruling, and acting of his own accord. The mere existence of Osiris, who is both dead and alive, tells us something important about superposition. The idea of something existing in two states at one long precedes our mathematical capabilities to prove it. Our natural intuition towards superposition in culture exists because we are part of nature and, as Feynman says, "if you want to make a simulation of nature, you'd better make it quantum mechanical."

Superposition is phenomenally important in many religions, including one that we are more contemperarily familiar with. 
![christianity](https://reflectionsonhim.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/09/holy-trinity.jpg)

Jesus and God (and the Holy Spirit). The Father and the Son are often attributed as one entity, yet somehow still separated. In the bible, it reads that "For in him (Jesus) the whole fullness of deity dwells" (Collosians 2:9). We examine Jesus as both human and divine, allowing both states to coexist in him at once. Christianity poses two crucial superpositions. The balance of divinity and humanity in Jesus, and the Father and Son sharing the same divine deification. The Christian deity exists in a superposition of Father and Son, an overseer unable to be seen, and a tangible man. This idea of God existing in multiple forms is the basis of the Holy Trinity, central to Christian ideology and theology, as well as one of the most well-known examples of cultural superposition.

On a broader scope, we can also see superposition in cultural movements. Renaissance Humanism, born in 15th-16th century Italy, had a distinctive character to it. One must be both secular and faithful. Worldly and holy. The Italian Renaissance was a time of developing science, but it was also a time where, especially in Italy, the Catholic church had massive influence over both day-to-day life and the distribution of wealth and power. This resulted in the glorification of both Catholic Doctrine and Dogma as well as more secular studies such as the liberal arts and classical literature. The courtier, ideally, would be someone who was both classically educated and the secular in his thoughts, glorifying the achievements of man, but also someone who lived for Jesus, and was dedicated to the Catholic faith. How were these men measured? By the metrics of their power and where they received it, as well as where their money went. Many classical Scholars ended up Cardinals or even popes, yet many men from extremely Catholic families and ended up serving their money to the Arts and the pursuit of historical recovery for writings of Cicero and art from ancient Greece. The manner truly maketh the man. The humanist movement displays how Quantum superposition plays into how we evaluate human characters. Two seemingly opposite states that must coexist, yet measured, revert to one or the other

## in totality, we cannot only consider Quantum superposition as a mathematical concept, but we can also not only consider it as a cultural concept. Quantum superposition exists in the society which discovers and utilizes it, and this comes with both a cultural context due to the mere fact that it exists in society, but it also comes from a long and prestigious history of mathematical discovery which allows us to understand it as much more than just existence in two states but a universal actor that unlocks new ways to view the universe and nature, as well as advance it scientifically.
